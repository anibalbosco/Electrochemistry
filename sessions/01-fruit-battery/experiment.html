Session 1 — Experiment: Fruit Battery

Session 1

Main experiment: Lemon/lime/potato battery — generating electricity from chemistry.

Figure 1 — Single galvanic cell: Zn anode (−), Cu cathode (+), electrons through the wire, ions through the fruit juice.

Part 1 — Build one cell (25–45 min)

Procedure

  1. Roll the lemon/lime firmly on the bench (breaks membranes, improves juice contact)
  2. Insert zinc electrode (galvanized nail or zinc strip) — do not pierce all the way through if avoidable
  3. Insert copper electrode (strip, wire, or penny) 2–3 cm away — electrodes must not touch
  4. Connect multimeter: red to copper, black to zinc (or follow meter convention; record which is which)
  5. Record voltage; note stability (does it drift down?)

Part 2 — Series battery challenge (45–60 min)

Figure 2 — Cells in series: connect Cu of one cell to Zn of the next; voltages add (~0.9 V each).

Procedure

  1. Build 2–3 identical cells (one fruit each)
  2. Connect in series: copper of cell 1 → zinc of cell 2 (via wire), etc.
  3. Measure total voltage across the free copper (positive end) and zinc (negative end)
  4. Compare to sum of individual cell voltages

Challenge question

"If each cell is 0.9 V, how many cells do you need for a 3 V buzzer? For a 1.5 V clock?"

Part 3 — Power something (60–75 min)

Load options (try in order of difficulty)

  1. Digital multimeter on current range (small load — should work)
  2. LED (needs correct polarity: longer leg = + ; try series with 3+ cells)
  3. Buzzer or low-voltage clock
  4. Small calculator (if available)

Discussion after attempt

  • Did voltage drop when connected to a load? (Internal resistance)
  • Why might the LED need more current than the lemon can supply?

Extensions — Side experiments (75–85 min)

Students choose one or more:

A. Compare fruits

Same electrodes, different fruits → compare voltage

B. Compare metals

Same fruit; try aluminum, steel nail, magnesium (if available) vs. zinc

C. pH changes

Use pH paper near each electrode after 10+ min operation

D. Salt addition

Add a pinch of salt to fruit — effect on voltage/current?

E. Parallel vs. series

Two cells in parallel — voltage vs. series?

End-of-session task — Copper solution prep (85–90 min)

Start overnight prep for Session 2 (see preparation.md):

  • ~100 mL white vinegar + 1–2 tsp table salt per jar
  • Add clean copper gaskets/wire with large surface area
  • Label jars; leave loosely covered overnight

Student handout — Quick reference

Build: Roll fruit → Zn + Cu, not touching → measure V
Series: + to − between cells → voltages add
Safety: Do not eat fruit; wash hands after handling metals
